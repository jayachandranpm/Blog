Every Single Moment is a Decision

Wednesday, December 17, 2025

Every Single Moment is a Decision

Right from when we wake up, we're making decisions. Hit snooze or get up? Check phone or not? Coffee or tea? Blue shirt or white? Every single action, every single moment.

And it's not just us. Everyone around us is deciding too. Your colleague deciding whether to speak up in that meeting. Your friend deciding to quit their job. That person at the traffic light deciding to wait or cross. We're all constantly observing, constantly deciding.

The Morning Chaos

Think about it. You wake up and boom - decisions start flooding in. Which side of the bed? Shower now or later? What to wear? Breakfast or skip it? Each one seems small but they add up. By the time you leave home, you've already made maybe 50-100 decisions and you haven't even started your actual day.

Most of these feel automatic. Like we're not even thinking. But we are. Every single one is a choice, a path taken, alternatives ignored.

Learning from Others

Here's what I find interesting - we learn so much just by watching. That colleague who always volunteers? They get promoted. That friend who keeps procrastinating? They miss opportunities. We're collecting data constantly about what works and what doesn't.

Even strangers teach us. Someone jaywalks while you wait for the signal - two different risk calculations, two different personalities, two different outcomes.

Not All Decisions Are Equal

Some decisions are trivial - which coffee to order. Some are massive - which career path to take, whether to commit to someone, how to handle a crisis.

The problem? We often treat them the same. I've seen people stress for 20 minutes over a menu but remain passive about their deteriorating career. Or someone impulsively making a life-changing decision they regret later.

Smart decision-makers seem to know the difference. Quick decisions on small stuff. Deep thinking on big stuff.

Decision Fatigue is Real

Here's the thing about modern life - we make WAY more decisions than our ancestors. A medieval person might make 20 significant choices a day. We make 20 before breakfast.

Every decision drains us. This is why Zuckerberg wears the same gray t-shirt. Why Obama stuck to blue or gray suits. They understood that deciding on clothes wastes the same mental energy needed for important stuff.

By 4pm, after hundreds of micro-choices, we're worse at deciding. More impulsive, more likely to stick with defaults, more paralyzed by options.

Too Many Options Problem

More choices should mean more freedom, right? Wrong. Research shows the opposite. 24 jam varieties? People buy less than when there are 6 options. More dating profiles? Less commitment. Infinite Netflix options? More scrolling, less watching.

And even when we finally choose, there's this nagging feeling - what if one of those other 23 options was better?

What's a Good Decision Anyway?

Is it about the outcome? The process? Alignment with values?

Here's my take - a good decision and a good outcome are different things. You can make a terrible decision and get lucky. You can make a smart decision and things still go wrong.

Good decisions are about the process. Did you gather enough info? Check your values? Assess risks honestly? Spend appropriate time thinking? The outcome often involves factors beyond your control.

Getting Better at Deciding

Decision-making is a skill. You can improve. Here's what works:

Know your decision types. Don't overthink breakfast. Save your energy for career moves.

Trust your gut selectively. Intuition works in areas where you have experience. It's dangerous in unfamiliar territory.

Look for reasons you're wrong. We naturally find evidence that supports what we already want. Force yourself to find counter-evidence.

Define your values early. If you've already decided family time is non-negotiable, saying no to that networking event becomes easier.

Accept imperfection. Waiting for perfect information means never deciding. Make a good-enough choice and adjust later.

Review and learn. After big decisions, examine your process. What did you consider? What did you miss? How did it turn out? Build your wisdom over time.

The Power in Deciding

Recognizing yourself as a decision-maker changes everything. You shift from passive to active. From victim to author. Your life is basically the sum of your choices.

That's heavy - the responsibility. But also liberating. If your choices created your current reality, your choices can create a different one.

Every morning you wake up and start again. Hundreds of decisions waiting. Some trivial, some huge, most somewhere in between. Each one is practice, each one nudges your life toward who you want to be.

The question isn't whether you'll make decisions today - you will, constantly. The question is whether you'll make them consciously, strategically, aligned with what matters to you, or let them just happen by default.

The choice, like always, is yours.
